# 🔍 The Problem & Our Solution

> **The world still relies on a 19th-century process to authenticate trillion-dollar documents.**

## Part 1: The Problem

### The Notarization System Is Completely Broken

Notarization — the official certification that a document is real, signed by the right parties, and legally binding — is required for:
- 🏠 Real estate transactions (deeds, titles, mortgages)
- ⚖️ Legal agreements (contracts, wills, power of attorney)
- 💼 Business filings (corporate documents, IP registrations)
- 🌍 International documents (passports, apostilles, visa applications)

These are not small or rare events. **Hundreds of millions of documents** need to be notarized every year in the United States alone.

Yet the system used to do this looks almost identical to how it was done 100 years ago:

---

### Problem #1: It's Slow and Inconvenient

Most notarizations require:
- Finding an available, licensed notary
- Scheduling an in-person appointment
- Driving to their office during business hours (typically 9–5, Monday–Friday)
- Waiting for the document to be processed
- For complex documents: waiting **days or weeks** for completion

This is completely unacceptable in 2025. You can file your taxes, sign a mortgage application, and transfer $1 million — all from your phone. But you still have to drive somewhere and meet a human in person to get a piece of paper stamped.

---

### Problem #2: It's Expensive

The costs add up quickly:
- Basic notarization fee: **$5 – $15 per signature**
- Complex documents (real estate, international): **$150 – $300+**
- Remote online notary (RON) services: **$25 – $100 per session**
- For businesses processing thousands of documents per month: **Tens of thousands of dollars in fees**

These costs are significant barriers, especially for small businesses, individuals in rural areas, and international transactions.

---

### Problem #3: It's Insecure and Easily Forged

This is the most alarming problem — and the one that costs the economy the most money.

**Forged notarizations are a massive and growing problem.** Because the current system relies on:
- A rubber stamp or embossed seal
- A handwritten signature
- A physical journal entry

...all of which can be faked with the right tools. Document fraud costs the US economy **billions of dollars per year**. Real estate deed fraud alone affected hundreds of thousands of homeowners in 2024, with criminals filing fake deeds to steal property titles.

Once a fraudulent document enters the system, it's almost impossible to track down because there is **no universal, accessible, tamper-proof record** that anyone can check.

---

### Problem #4: High-Value Documents Are Financial "Dead Weight"

This is the problem most people don't think about — but it represents the biggest financial opportunity.

A commercial real estate deed, an intellectual property portfolio, or a large government contract can be worth **millions or tens of millions of dollars.** But once it's notarized and filed, that value just... sits there. You can't easily:
- Sell part of it
- Borrow against it efficiently
- Let other investors participate in its growth
- Trade it on a marketplace

These assets are **illiquid** — you either own the whole thing or you don't. This locks thousands of high-value assets into the hands of a tiny number of wealthy players who can afford to buy them outright.

**Here's why this matters:**

This artwork isn't decorative. The specific patterns, colors, and shapes in the image are mathematically calculated using the contents of the document itself. It's like a fingerprint — no two documents produce the same artwork, and changing even a single word in the document would produce a completely different image.

Anyone — a bank, a court, a foreign government, a private individual — can **scan the artwork** to instantly verify:

- Has it been altered since it was notarized?
- When was it notarized?
- Who signed it?

And they can do all of this **for free, from anywhere in the world, with no account or special software required.** This is truly global, universal document verification — for the first time, ever.
